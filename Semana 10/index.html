It all started with wanting to play with texture and turn it into a typeface, literally.

The child’s toy “Moco Loco”, which is basically a green slime, was the source material for Blob. It was stretched and pushed, manipulated to become a specific letter which could only be photographed in about ten seconds because the material would begin to return to its original shape.

After all the letters, numbers and special characters were photographed, they were vectorized to become a digital typeface that everyone can use to funk up their type archive.

When making the type specimen, there was an obvious and accurate way of presenting the typography, a “Nickelodeon” approach. This seemed a bit too effortless and cliché, and so the search began for a way to apply the type that was interesting and also related closely to the type and its anatomy.

Then the 1958 horror film, The Blob, came to mind. Taking graphic elements from the original movie poster (the illustration of the woman) and taking as inspiration the general style of the posters of that era, Blob is showcased as a type for attention-calling headers.

Though Blob can be as friendly as Flubber, it can also be a horrofying, shapeless creature.
